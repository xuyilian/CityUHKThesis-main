\begin{abstract}

This thesis presents a novel water-skipping micro aerial vehicle designed to move across the water surface through high-speed rotation and hydrofoil impact. Inspired by stone-skipping, the vehicle uses rotating hydrofoils to generate lift when contacting the water, enabling it to bounce upward after impact.

A dynamic model is developed using a quasi-steady method and blade element theory to analyze the water-skipping process. Simulations are conducted to study how rotational speed and hydrofoil angle affect the jumping performance. Based on the results, an integrated 3D-printed UAV structure is designed, combining the main body and hydrofoil components into a single lightweight frame. Waterproof electronics are also incorporated to support repeated water-contact experiments.

To achieve the required high-speed rotation, the vehicle can operate in both quadcopter and bicopter modes. A flight controller is developed to stabilize the vehicle, especially in bicopter mode where the system is underactuated. In addition, experiments are conducted in a motion-capture environment to evaluate the water-skipping performance. The results demonstrate that the vehicle can achieve controlled constant-height jumping on the water surface, validating the feasibility of the proposed design and control strategy.

This work provides a foundation for developing aerial robots capable of operating near and on water surfaces.
\end{abstract}
