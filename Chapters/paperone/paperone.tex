\chapter{Design of the Water-Skipping MAV}
\label{chapter:paperone}

\section{Design Requirements}
\label{sec:design_requirements}

The vehicle must satisfy two coupled sets of requirements: it must generate
enough lift to hover as a quadrotor, and its spinning hydrofoils must produce
sufficient hydrodynamic impulse during a brief water-surface contact to eject
it back into the air.

\paragraph{Flight requirements.}
The target all-up mass is $m = 70\;\mathrm{g}$.  All four propellers spin in
the same direction, so yaw torque cannot be independently cancelled; the
vehicle instead reaches the relaxed-hover equilibrium spin rate
$\omega_z^*$ derived in Chapter~\ref{chapter:papertwo}.  The design target
spin rate is approximately $66\;\mathrm{rad\,s^{-1}}$, set by the interplay of
propeller reaction torque and hydrofoil profile drag (equation~\eqref{eq:omegaz_hover}).
The four rotors are driven from a single-cell (\SI{3.7}{V} nominal) lithium
polymer battery, which constrains motor selection to high-KV, small-diameter
units compatible with the propeller geometry.

\paragraph{Water-skipping requirements.}
For a successful hop the hydrofoils must, during the brief submersion phase,
generate a net vertical impulse large enough to reverse the vehicle's downward
velocity and return it to free flight.  Blade-element simulation of the
submersion dynamics (described in Chapter~\ref{chapter:dynamic}) shows that
both the hydrofoil inclination angle $\beta$ and the spin rate $\omega_z^*$
at water contact are primary drivers of the ejection velocity; the design
therefore cannot be finalised independently of the flight dynamics.  In
addition, the foils must not generate so much profile drag during flight
that $\omega_z^*$ falls outside the controllable regime of
Chapter~\ref{chapter:papertwo}.

\section{Overall Mechanical Design}
\label{sec:mech_design}

\paragraph{Frame and propulsion.}
The frame is an X-configuration quadrotor with arm length $l =
50\;\mathrm{mm}$ from the centre of mass to each motor mount.  Four
\mbox{0802} motors rated at $22{,}000\;\mathrm{KV}$ are installed at the arm
tips, all rotating in the same direction.  Each motor drives a two-blade
propeller of $50\;\mathrm{mm}$ diameter.  The propeller tip circle therefore
extends to $l + r_p = 50 + 25 = 75\;\mathrm{mm}$ from the vehicle centre.

\paragraph{Electronics.}
The flight controller is a Crazyflie\;2.x Brushless board, which integrates a
six-axis IMU, a radio transceiver, and motor drivers on a single compact
module.  Power is supplied by a $1\mathrm{S}$ ($3.7\;\mathrm{V}$)
lithium polymer cell of capacity $550\;\mathrm{mAh}$, mounted centrally to
minimise the shift of the centre of mass.  The complete vehicle has an all-up
mass of $m = 70\;\mathrm{g}$.

\paragraph{Hydrofoil mounting.}
Each hydrofoil is mounted below the frame body at a $45^{\circ}$ azimuthal
offset from the nearest motor arm, positioning it midway between adjacent
propellers.  This offset ensures that the downward propeller wash passes
between the foils rather than directly onto them, avoiding aerodynamic
interference between the propellers and the hydrofoil surfaces during
hovering flight.  The foil tip is at radius $r_f = 70\;\mathrm{mm}$ from
the vehicle centre---$20\;\mathrm{mm}$ beyond the motor arm length of
$50\;\mathrm{mm}$---placing it just inside the propeller tip circle and
entirely beneath the propeller plane.  The four-foil assembly rotates at
the vehicle yaw rate $\omega_z$ and acts collectively as a single large
rotor disk of radius $r_f$, as described in
Section~\ref{sec:attitude_dynamics}.

\section{Hydrofoil Geometry Design}
\label{sec:foil_design}

\paragraph{Profile and planform.}
Each hydrofoil is a thin flat rectangular plate, chosen for simplicity of
fabrication and because the flat-plate aerodynamic model (lift coefficient
$C_L = 2\sin\alpha\cos\alpha$, drag coefficient $C_D = 2\sin^2\alpha$)
provides a closed-form basis for the blade-element simulation of
Chapter~\ref{chapter:dynamic}.  The foil extends radially inward from the tip
radius $r_\mathrm{tip} = 70\;\mathrm{mm}$ and is parametrised by its planform
area $S_f$ and its chord $c_f$ (the dimension tangential to the sweep
direction).  For a rectangular foil the radial span is $S_f/c_f$, so the root
radius is
\begin{equation}
    r_\mathrm{root} = r_\mathrm{tip} - \frac{S_f}{c_f}.
    \label{eq:foil_root}
\end{equation}
Holding the planform area $S_f$ fixed while varying the chord therefore trades
chord against span: a wider chord yields a shorter, stubbier foil and a
narrower chord a longer, slender one.% TODO: state final S_f and c_f from as-built foils

\paragraph{Collective pitch and design selection.}
The hydrofoil is mounted at a fixed collective pitch angle $\beta$ relative to
the horizontal plane.  A blade-element parameter sweep over inclination angles
$\beta \in \{5^{\circ}, 10^{\circ}, 15^{\circ}, 20^{\circ}, 25^{\circ},
30^{\circ}\}$ and chord lengths $c_f \in \{150, 200, 350\}\;\mathrm{mm}$
(Chapter~\ref{chapter:dynamic}) identifies the combinations that yield
sufficient ejection velocity while maintaining the target $\omega_z^*$.% TODO: state chosen beta and c_f from experimental results

Each candidate geometry is realised as a separate fabricated foil set built at
its specific chord and pitch; geometries are compared by swapping complete
sets between test runs.  No active or passive pitch adjustment mechanism is
implemented, and the collective pitch is fixed at manufacture for each foil.
