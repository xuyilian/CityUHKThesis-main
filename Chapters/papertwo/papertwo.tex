\section{Coordinate Frames and System Definition}
\label{sec:coord_frames}

The flight dynamics and control framework presented in this chapter follows
the formulation of \textcite{bai2020splitflyer,bai2022splitflyerair} for
rotating underactuated rotorcraft, adapted to the present vehicle.  The
critical design feature distinguishing this quadrotor from a conventional
multirotor is that \emph{all four propellers spin in the same direction}.
The individual aerodynamic yaw torques therefore do not cancel, and the
vehicle sustains a non-zero net yaw torque during flight.  At equilibrium
this drives the vehicle to a constant spinning yaw rate---the
\emph{relaxed hovering condition}~\cite{mueller2016relaxed,bai2020splitflyer}---which
fundamentally shapes the dynamics and motivates the gyroscopic analysis that
follows.

\paragraph{Inertial and body frames.}
The right-handed inertial frame $(\hat{x}^w, \hat{y}^w, \hat{z}^w)$ is
fixed to the ground with $\hat{z}^w$ pointing vertically upward.  The
position of the vehicle's centre of mass (COM) in this frame is
$\boldsymbol{p} = [x,\,y,\,z]^{\top}$.  The right-handed body frame
$(\hat{x}^b, \hat{y}^b, \hat{z}^b)$ is rigidly attached to the vehicle with
its origin at the COM; the $\hat{z}^b$ axis is aligned with the common
thrust axis of the four propellers.  The rotation matrix
$\boldsymbol{R}\in SO(3)$ maps body-frame vectors to the inertial frame,
so the world-frame direction of the thrust axis is $\boldsymbol{R}\hat{e}_3$,
where $\hat{e}_3 = [0,0,1]^{\top}$.  The body angular velocity expressed in
the body frame is $\boldsymbol{\omega} = [\omega_x, \omega_y, \omega_z]^{\top}$.
The yaw angle $\psi$ is defined as the rotation of $\hat{x}^b$ about
$\hat{z}^w$ relative to $\hat{x}^w$.

\paragraph{Non-revolving frame.}
Because the vehicle spins at high yaw rate, a non-revolving intermediate
frame $(\hat{x}^m, \hat{y}^m, \hat{z}^b)$ is introduced to describe the
attitude of the spinning disk independently of the instantaneous yaw
angle~\cite{bai2022splitflyerair}.  Under the small-inclination assumption
$|\alpha_x|, |\alpha_y| \ll 1$, the reduced attitude state
$\boldsymbol{\eta} = [\alpha_x, \alpha_y]^{\top}$ parametrises the deviation
of $\hat{z}^b$ from vertical, and the non-revolving axes are approximated as
\begin{equation}
    \hat{x}^m \approx [1,\;0,\;{-\alpha_y}]^{\top},
    \qquad
    \hat{y}^m \approx [0,\;1,\;\alpha_x]^{\top}.
    \label{eq:nm_frame}
\end{equation}
The pair $(\hat{x}^m, \hat{y}^m)$ spans the horizontal plane for any yaw
angle $\psi$; this frame is the natural setting for the gyroscopic reduced
attitude dynamics derived in Section~\ref{sec:reduced_dynamics}.

\paragraph{Propeller geometry.}
The four propellers are arranged in an X-configuration at equal arm length
$l$ from the COM.  Following the convention of
\textcite{bai2022splitflyerair}, their body-frame position vectors are
\begin{equation}
    \boldsymbol{l}_1 = -\boldsymbol{l}_3
        = \frac{l}{\sqrt{2}}\!\left(\hat{x}^b + \hat{y}^b\right),
    \qquad
    \boldsymbol{l}_2 = -\boldsymbol{l}_4
        = \frac{l}{\sqrt{2}}\!\left(\hat{x}^b - \hat{y}^b\right).
    \label{eq:prop_locs}
\end{equation}
Each propeller $i$ produces a propulsive thrust $f_{i,p} \geq 0$ directed
along $+\hat{z}^b$ and an aerodynamic reaction torque $c\,f_{i,p}$ about
$\hat{z}^b$, where $c > 0$ is the common thrust-to-drag ratio (identical
for all four rotors since they share the same spinning direction).
Consequently the total yaw torque is
\begin{equation}
    \tau_z = c\sum_{i=1}^{4} f_{i,p} = c\,F,
    \label{eq:yaw_torque}
\end{equation}
where $F = \sum_i f_{i,p}$ is the total thrust.  Equation~\eqref{eq:yaw_torque}
shows that the yaw torque is always coupled to the total thrust and cannot
be nullified independently.  This coupling drives the vehicle passively to a
constant spin rate---the \emph{relaxed hovering condition}~\cite{mueller2016relaxed}---whose
equilibrium value and dependence on vehicle mass and air density are derived
in Section~\ref{sec:attitude_dynamics}.

\paragraph{Inertia and drag.}
By design, mass is distributed symmetrically about the $\hat{z}^b$ axis so
that the moments of inertia about roll and pitch are approximately equal,
$I_x \approx I_y \equiv I_d$.  The full inertia tensor is therefore
\begin{equation}
    \boldsymbol{I} = \mathrm{diag}(I_d,\;I_d,\;I_z),
    \label{eq:inertia}
\end{equation}
which is the axisymmetric condition required to treat the vehicle as a
gyroscope in Section~\ref{sec:reduced_dynamics}.  Rotor-induced drag is
characterised by the coefficient matrix
$\boldsymbol{B} = \mathrm{diag}(B_h, B_h, B_v)$, and aerodynamic body
damping by $\boldsymbol{D} = \mathrm{diag}(D_h, D_h, D_v)$, where subscripts
$h$ and $v$ denote horizontal and vertical components
respectively~\cite{bai2022splitflyerair}.  The vehicle mass is $m = 70\;\mathrm{g}$;
remaining hardware parameters are specified in Chapter~\ref{chapter:paperone}.

\paragraph{Propeller aerodynamic model.}
Each propeller $i$ spins about its own thrust axis at blade speed $\Omega_i$,
which is distinct from and substantially larger than the vehicle yaw rate
$\omega_z$.  The propulsive thrust follows the standard aerodynamic model
\begin{equation}
    f_{i,p} = k_f\,\Omega_i^2,
    \label{eq:thrust_model}
\end{equation}
where $k_f > 0$ is a propeller constant.  Section~\ref{sec:attitude_dynamics}
introduces an additional aerodynamic effect absent from conventional
multirotor models: an advancing/retreating blade moment arising because the
four hydrofoils, attached at the arm tips, sweep through the air as the
entire vehicle yaws.  The hydrofoil assembly acts as a single large rotor
disk of radius $l$ rotating at the vehicle yaw rate $\omega_z$; the analysis
introduces the hydrofoil advancing/retreating blade coefficient $K_{AB}$,
parameterised by the hydrofoil chord $c_f$, collective pitch $\theta_f$,
two-dimensional lift slope $a_0$, and arm length $l$.

\section{Translational Dynamics}
\label{sec:translational_dynamics}

The translational dynamics follow directly from the framework of
\textcite{bai2022splitflyerair} and are unchanged by the all-same-direction
propeller configuration.  Treating the vehicle as a rigid body of mass $m$,
Newton's second law gives
\begin{equation}
    m\ddot{\boldsymbol{p}} = \boldsymbol{R}\sum_{i=1}^{4}\boldsymbol{f}_i
        - mg\hat{e}_3,
    \label{eq:trans_eom}
\end{equation}
where the total force from propeller $i$ is
\begin{equation}
    \boldsymbol{f}_i = \hat{e}_3 f_{i,p} + \boldsymbol{f}_{i,d}.
    \label{eq:prop_force}
\end{equation}
The propulsive thrust $f_{i,p}$ is directed along the body $\hat{z}^b$ axis.
The rotor-induced drag $\boldsymbol{f}_{i,d}$, modelled as a linear function
of the air velocity at the propeller disk~\cite{bai2022splitflyerair}, is
\begin{equation}
    \boldsymbol{f}_{i,d} = -\boldsymbol{B}
        \!\left(\boldsymbol{R}^{-1}\dot{\boldsymbol{p}}
        + \boldsymbol{\omega}\times\boldsymbol{l}_i\right),
    \label{eq:rotor_drag}
\end{equation}
where the first term accounts for the translational velocity of the vehicle
and the second for the velocity of the propeller hub due to body rotation.

\paragraph{Cancellation of rotation-induced drag.}
The symmetry of the X-configuration---$\boldsymbol{l}_1 + \boldsymbol{l}_2
+ \boldsymbol{l}_3 + \boldsymbol{l}_4 = \mathbf{0}$, which follows
immediately from~\eqref{eq:prop_locs}---ensures that the rotation-induced
drag terms cancel when summed:
\begin{equation}
    \sum_{i=1}^{4}\left(\boldsymbol{\omega}\times\boldsymbol{l}_i\right)
        = \boldsymbol{\omega}\times\sum_{i=1}^{4}\boldsymbol{l}_i
        = \mathbf{0}.
    \label{eq:drag_cancel}
\end{equation}
This cancellation holds regardless of propeller spin direction and therefore
applies equally to our vehicle.  Substituting into~\eqref{eq:trans_eom}:
\begin{equation}
    m\ddot{\boldsymbol{p}}
        = \boldsymbol{R}\hat{e}_3 F
          - 4\boldsymbol{R}\boldsymbol{B}\boldsymbol{R}^{-1}\dot{\boldsymbol{p}}
          - mg\hat{e}_3.
    \label{eq:trans_full}
\end{equation}
The second term is a translational damping force present in all multirotor
vehicles.  For non-aggressive near-hover flight it is small relative to
gravity and is neglected in what follows~\cite{bai2022splitflyerair}, yielding
\begin{equation}
    m\ddot{\boldsymbol{p}} \approx \boldsymbol{R}\hat{e}_3 F - mg\hat{e}_3.
    \label{eq:trans_hover}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Note on the advancing/retreating blade effect.}
The advancing/retreating blade moment derived in
Section~\ref{sec:attitude_dynamics} arises from the spinning hydrofoil
assembly generating an asymmetric vertical lift distribution when the vehicle
translates horizontally.  The first-harmonic lift asymmetry produces zero net
horizontal force by symmetry of the four-foil X-configuration
(the $\sum_i\sin\phi_i = \sum_i\cos\phi_i = 0$ identities cancel the
horizontal components), so the effect manifests solely as a torque about the
vehicle COM and does not appear in~\eqref{eq:trans_eom}.  The translational
dynamics~\eqref{eq:trans_hover} are therefore unaffected.

\paragraph{Near-hover linearisation.}
For small inclinations $|\alpha_x|,|\alpha_y|\ll 1$, the body $\hat{z}^b$
axis expressed in the inertial frame is approximately
\begin{equation}
    \boldsymbol{R}\hat{e}_3 \approx
        \begin{bmatrix} J\boldsymbol{\eta} \\ 1 \end{bmatrix},
    \qquad
    J = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ -1 & 0 \end{bmatrix},
    \label{eq:zb_approx}
\end{equation}
where $J$ is the $-90^{\circ}$ rotation matrix in the horizontal plane.
Splitting~\eqref{eq:trans_hover} into horizontal and vertical components
and using the hover thrust condition $F\approx mg$ for the horizontal term
gives
\begin{align}
    m\ddot{\boldsymbol{p}}_2 &= mg\,J\boldsymbol{\eta},
    \label{eq:horiz_dyn}\\
    m\ddot{z} &= F - mg,
    \label{eq:vert_dyn}
\end{align}
where $\boldsymbol{p}_2 = [x,\,y]^{\top}$ is the horizontal position.
Equation~\eqref{eq:horiz_dyn} shows that horizontal acceleration is driven
solely by the vehicle tilt $\boldsymbol{\eta}$---the same result as for a
conventional quadrotor or the SplitFlyer bicopter---and forms the basis of
the position controller in Section~\ref{sec:controller}.

\section{Attitude Dynamics}
\label{sec:attitude_dynamics}

The rotational motion is governed by Euler's equations in the body frame.
Following the framework of \textcite{bai2022splitflyerair}, the angular
momentum equation is
\begin{equation}
    \boldsymbol{I}\dot{\boldsymbol{\omega}}
        + \boldsymbol{\omega}\times\boldsymbol{I}\boldsymbol{\omega}
        = \sum_{i=1}^{4}\boldsymbol{\tau}_i
          + \boldsymbol{\tau}_s,
    \label{eq:euler}
\end{equation}
where $\boldsymbol{\tau}_i$ is the net torque produced by propeller $i$
at the centre of mass, and $\boldsymbol{\tau}_s = -\boldsymbol{D}\boldsymbol{\omega}$
is the aerodynamic body damping.

\paragraph{Propeller torques.}
The total torque from propeller $i$ decomposes into a thrust-induced
component and a drag-induced component:
\begin{equation}
    \boldsymbol{\tau}_i = \boldsymbol{\tau}_{i,p} + \boldsymbol{\tau}_{i,d}.
    \label{eq:prop_torque_decomp}
\end{equation}
The thrust-induced torque combines the moment of the propulsive force about
the centre of mass with the aerodynamic reaction torque of the spinning
blade~\cite{bai2022splitflyerair}:
\begin{equation}
    \boldsymbol{\tau}_{i,p}
        = \boldsymbol{l}_i \times f_{i,p}\hat{e}_3
          + c\,f_{i,p}\hat{e}_3.
    \label{eq:tau_thrust}
\end{equation}
The first term generates the differential roll/pitch torques used for
attitude control; the second is the yaw reaction torque.  Summing the yaw
contributions across all four propellers recovers~\eqref{eq:yaw_torque},
showing that yaw remains coupled to the total thrust and is not independently
actuated.

The drag-induced torque arises from the rotor drag force
$\boldsymbol{f}_{i,d}$ (defined in~\eqref{eq:rotor_drag}) acting at the
moment arm $\boldsymbol{l}_i$:
\begin{equation}
    \boldsymbol{\tau}_{i,d}
        = \boldsymbol{l}_i \times \boldsymbol{f}_{i,d}
        = -\boldsymbol{l}_i
            \times \boldsymbol{B}
            \!\left(\boldsymbol{R}^{-1}\dot{\boldsymbol{p}}
                  + \boldsymbol{\omega}\times\boldsymbol{l}_i\right).
    \label{eq:tau_drag}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Advancing/retreating blade moment.}
The hydrofoils are attached at the four arm tips at equal radius $l$ from
the vehicle COM.  As the vehicle spins at yaw rate $\omega_z$, each hydrofoil
sweeps tangentially at speed $\omega_z l$.  The four-foil assembly therefore
functions aerodynamically as a single large rotor disk of radius $l$ rotating
at $\omega_z$---a vehicle-scale rotor distinct from the small propellers.

When the vehicle translates horizontally, this large rotor experiences the
classical advancing/retreating blade asymmetry.  Let
$\boldsymbol{v}_b = (\boldsymbol{R}^{-1}\dot{\boldsymbol{p}})_\perp$ denote
the horizontal translational velocity expressed in the body frame.  At foil
$i$, located at azimuth $\phi_i$ from $+\hat{x}^b$ and tangential direction
$\hat{\boldsymbol{t}}_i = (-\sin\phi_i,\,\cos\phi_i,\,0)^{\top}$, the total
tangential airspeed is
\begin{equation}
    U_i = \omega_z l + \hat{\boldsymbol{t}}_i\cdot\boldsymbol{v}_b.
    \label{eq:foil_speed}
\end{equation}
The first term $\omega_z l$ is the primary sweep speed; the second is the
translational perturbation that breaks the rotational symmetry.  Modelling
each hydrofoil as a flat plate with chord $c_f$, collective pitch $\theta_f$,
and two-dimensional lift slope $a_0$, the aerodynamic lift in air is
\begin{equation}
    L_i = k_{HF}\,U_i^2,
    \qquad
    k_{HF} = \tfrac{1}{2}\rho\,c_f\,a_0\,\theta_f.
    \label{eq:foil_lift}
\end{equation}
The moment about the COM from the vertical lift force $L_i\hat{e}_3$ at
arm position $l_i = l(\cos\phi_i,\,\sin\phi_i,\,0)^{\top}$ is
\begin{equation}
    \boldsymbol{\tau}_i = L_i\,l
        \bigl(\sin\phi_i\,\hat{x}^b - \cos\phi_i\,\hat{y}^b\bigr).
    \label{eq:foil_moment_i}
\end{equation}
Expanding $U_i^2 \approx (\omega_z l)^2 + 2\omega_z l\,(\hat{\boldsymbol{t}}_i\cdot\boldsymbol{v}_b)$
to first order in $|\boldsymbol{v}_b|/(\omega_z l)$ and summing over the
four X-configuration foils ($\phi_i = 45^{\circ}, 135^{\circ}, 225^{\circ},
315^{\circ}$), the zeroth-order terms cancel by the identities
$\sum_i\sin\phi_i = \sum_i\cos\phi_i = 0$, and the first-order cross-terms
combine using $\sum_i\sin^2\phi_i = \sum_i\cos^2\phi_i = 2$ and
$\sum_i\sin\phi_i\cos\phi_i = 0$.  The result is
\begin{equation}
    \boldsymbol{\tau}_{AB}
        = -K_{AB}
            \bigl(\boldsymbol{R}^{-1}\dot{\boldsymbol{p}}\bigr)_\perp,
    \label{eq:tau_ab}
\end{equation}
where the \emph{hydrofoil advancing/retreating blade coefficient} is
\begin{equation}
    K_{AB} = 4\,k_{HF}\,\omega_z\,l^2
           = 2\rho\,c_f\,a_0\,\theta_f\,\omega_z\,l^2.
    \label{eq:KAB}
\end{equation}
The coefficient $K_{AB}$ scales with $\omega_z$: the faster the vehicle
spins (heavier vehicle or thinner air, from~\eqref{eq:omegaz_hover}), the
stronger the translation--attitude coupling.  The negative sign
in~\eqref{eq:tau_ab} indicates that the moment opposes the translational
velocity in the body frame: when the vehicle moves in $+\hat{x}^b$, the
foil at $\phi_i = 270^{\circ}$ (advancing, on the $-\hat{y}^b$ side for
$c > 0$) generates more lift, rolling the vehicle toward $-\hat{x}^b$.
This translation--attitude coupling is absent from conventional multirotor
models and propagates into the reduced attitude dynamics of
Section~\ref{sec:reduced_dynamics}.

\paragraph{Complete Euler equation.}
Collecting all torque contributions, with the propeller torques
$\boldsymbol{\tau}_{i,p}$ and $\boldsymbol{\tau}_{i,d}$ acting at each
rotor hub and the hydrofoil moment $\boldsymbol{\tau}_{AB}$ acting on the
vehicle as a whole:
\begin{equation}
    \boldsymbol{I}\dot{\boldsymbol{\omega}}
        + \boldsymbol{\omega}\times\boldsymbol{I}\boldsymbol{\omega}
        = \sum_{i=1}^{4}
            \bigl(\boldsymbol{\tau}_{i,p}
                  + \boldsymbol{\tau}_{i,d}\bigr)
          + \boldsymbol{\tau}_{AB}
          + \boldsymbol{\tau}_s.
    \label{eq:euler_full}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Passive yaw equilibrium.}
The hydrofoil advancing/retreating blade torque $\boldsymbol{\tau}_{AB}$
lies entirely in the body $\hat{x}^b$--$\hat{y}^b$ plane and carries no
$\hat{z}^b$ component.  The dominant source of yaw drag in this vehicle is
the aerodynamic profile drag of the four hydrofoils as they sweep through air.
At yaw rate $\omega_z$, each foil at arm radius $l$ moves at tangential
speed $\omega_z l$.  Its profile drag, acting opposite to the sweep direction,
is
\begin{equation}
    D_{f,i} = k_{D,f}\,(\omega_z l)^2,
    \qquad
    k_{D,f} = \tfrac{1}{2}\rho\,C_{D,f}\,S_f,
    \label{eq:foil_drag}
\end{equation}
where $C_{D,f}$ is the hydrofoil profile drag coefficient and $S_f$ is the
foil planform area.  Each drag force acts at moment arm $l$ about the
$\hat{z}^b$ axis, contributing a yaw-retarding torque $-k_{D,f}l^3\omega_z^2$
per foil.  Extracting the $\hat{z}^b$ projection of~\eqref{eq:euler_full},
using $[\boldsymbol{\omega}\times\boldsymbol{I}\boldsymbol{\omega}]_z = 0$
(axisymmetric inertia), and collecting all four foil drag torques gives the
yaw dynamics
\begin{equation}
    I_z\dot{\omega}_z
        = c\,F
          - 4\,k_{D,f}\,l^3\,\omega_z^2.
    \label{eq:yaw_ode}
\end{equation}
The quadratic damping term arises directly from the foil geometry and air
density through $k_{D,f}$; it creates a unique stable equilibrium at
$\omega_z^* > 0$.  At sustained hover, $F = mg$, giving the
\emph{relaxed hover spin rate}
\begin{equation}
    \boxed{\omega_z^* = \sqrt{\frac{c\,m\,g}{4\,k_{D,f}\,l^3}}.}
    \label{eq:omegaz_hover}
\end{equation}
This equilibrium is reached entirely passively: the spinning hydrofoils
generate exactly the drag needed to balance the propeller yaw torque, with
no active yaw input required.

\paragraph{Dependence on vehicle mass and air density.}
Two scaling properties of~\eqref{eq:omegaz_hover} are worth noting.
First, $\omega_z^*$ increases with vehicle weight $mg$.  A heavier vehicle
requires greater thrust to hover, increasing the yaw drive torque $cF = cmg$
while the foil drag geometry is unchanged.  Counterintuitively, a heavier
vehicle spins \emph{faster} than a geometrically identical lighter one; the
square-root relationship means doubling the mass raises the spin rate by a
factor of $\sqrt{2}$.

Second, the foil drag coefficient $k_{D,f} = \frac{1}{2}\rho C_{D,f}S_f$
scales linearly with air density $\rho$.  The thrust-to-drag ratio $c =
k_\tau/k_f$ is the ratio of two $\rho$-proportional quantities and is
therefore independent of $\rho$.  Consequently
\begin{equation}
    \omega_z^* \propto \sqrt{\frac{mg}{\rho\,C_{D,f}\,S_f\,l^3}},
    \label{eq:omegaz_scaling}
\end{equation}
so the equilibrium spin rate is set by the vehicle weight, the foil drag
geometry, and the local air density---none of which requires active control.
At higher altitude (lower $\rho$) the profile drag is weaker and the vehicle
spins faster.  This constant $\omega_z^*$ is the spin rate assumed in
Section~\ref{sec:reduced_dynamics}.
