\chapter{Dynamic Modeling of Water-Skipping Motion}

\label{dynamic}

\section{Overview of Water-Skipping Process}

\section{Quasi-Steady Hydrodynamic Model}

During the water-skipping process, the rotating hydrofoil impacts the water surface and generates hydrodynamic lift and drag. Since the density of water is much larger than that of air, the hydrodynamic force during water contact dominates the motion of the vehicle. Therefore, in this model, only the force generated by the submerged hydrofoil is considered.

Figure~\ref{fig:quasi_steady_model} shows the quasi-steady hydrodynamic model of a hydrofoil during water impact. The hydrofoil moves with a horizontal velocity component $v_x$, which is mainly induced by the rotation of the vehicle, and a vertical velocity component $\dot{z}$, which is caused by the falling motion of the vehicle. The resultant velocity of the hydrofoil relative to water is denoted as $v$.


The resultant velocity can be expressed as

\begin{equation}
    v = \sqrt{v_x^2 + \dot{z}^2}.
    \label{eq:resultant_velocity}
\end{equation}

The angle between the resultant velocity and the horizontal direction is defined as $\gamma$, which is given by

\begin{equation}
    \gamma = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{-\dot{z}}{v_x}\right).
    \label{eq:gamma}
\end{equation}

Here, $\dot{z}<0$ represents downward motion before impact. Therefore, $-\dot{z}$ is used to represent the downward velocity magnitude. The hydrofoil is installed with an inclination angle $\beta$ relative to the horizontal direction. As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:quasi_steady_model}, the angle of attack $\alpha$ is determined by both the hydrofoil inclination angle and the incoming flow direction. Thus,

\begin{equation}
    \alpha = \beta + \gamma.
    \label{eq:alpha}
\end{equation}

Based on the quasi-steady assumption, the instantaneous hydrodynamic lift and drag generated by the hydrofoil can be written as

\begin{equation}
    F_L = \frac{1}{2}\rho C_L S v^2,
    \label{eq:lift_force}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
    F_D = \frac{1}{2}\rho C_D S v^2,
    \label{eq:drag_force}
\end{equation}

where $\rho$ is the density of water, $S$ is the submerged area of the hydrofoil, and $C_L$ and $C_D$ are the lift and drag coefficients, respectively. The lift force $F_L$ is perpendicular to the relative flow direction, while the drag force $F_D$ acts along the direction opposite to the relative flow.

For a flat-plate hydrofoil, the lift and drag coefficients can be approximated by

\begin{equation}
    C_L = 2 \sin{\alpha}\cos{\alpha},
    \label{eq:cl}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
    C_D = 2 \sin^2{\alpha},
    \label{eq:cd}
\end{equation}

\section{Blade Element Method}

The quasi-steady hydrodynamic model describes the lift and drag generated by a hydrofoil with a given velocity and angle of attack. However, for a rotating hydrofoil, the linear velocity is not uniform along the span, since each section has a different distance from the rotational center. Therefore, the blade element method is used to calculate the hydrodynamic force distribution along the hydrofoil.

As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:blade_element_model}, the hydrofoil is mounted on the rotating body and extends from the root radius $r_{\mathrm{root}}$ to the tip radius $r_{\mathrm{tip}}$. A small blade element located at radius $r$ has a radial length $dr$ and chord length $c$. The tangential velocity, resultant velocity, inflow angle, and local angle of attack of the blade element are given by

\begin{equation}
    v_x(r) = \omega r,
    \label{eq:blade_tangential_velocity}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
    v(r) = \sqrt{(\omega r)^2 + \dot{z}^{\,2}},
    \label{eq:blade_resultant_velocity}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
    \gamma(r) = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{-\dot{z}}{\omega r}\right),
    \label{eq:blade_gamma}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
    \alpha(r) = \beta + \gamma(r),
    \label{eq:blade_alpha}
\end{equation}

where $\omega$ is the angular velocity of the vehicle, $\dot{z}$ is the vertical velocity, and $\beta$ is the hydrofoil inclination angle.

For a small blade element, the submerged area is

\begin{equation}
    dS = c\,dr .
    \label{eq:blade_area}
\end{equation}

Based on the quasi-steady assumption, the differential lift and drag generated by this blade element are

\begin{equation}
    dF_L = \frac{1}{2}\rho C_L(\alpha) v^2(r)c\,dr ,
    \label{eq:differential_lift}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
    dF_D = \frac{1}{2}\rho C_D(\alpha) v^2(r)c\,dr ,
    \label{eq:differential_drag}
\end{equation}

where $\rho$ is the density of water, and $C_L(\alpha)$ and $C_D(\alpha)$ are the lift and drag coefficients.

The differential vertical thrust and horizontal resistance force are obtained by decomposing the lift and drag forces:

\begin{equation}
    dT = dF_L \cos{\gamma(r)} + dF_D \sin{\gamma(r)} ,
    \label{eq:differential_thrust}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
    dF_R = dF_D \cos{\gamma(r)} - dF_L \sin{\gamma(r)} .
    \label{eq:differential_resistance}
\end{equation}

The horizontal resistance force produces a resisting torque about the rotational center:

\begin{equation}
    dQ = r\,dF_R
    =
    r\left(dF_D \cos{\gamma(r)} - dF_L \sin{\gamma(r)}\right).
    \label{eq:differential_torque}
\end{equation}


\section{Vertical Motion and Rotational Dynamics}

The total vertical thrust and resisting torque generated by one hydrofoil can be obtained by integrating the blade elements along the radial direction. In this study, the total hydrofoil area $S$ and the tip radius $r_{\mathrm{tip}}$ are assumed to be known, while the chord length $c$ is treated as a design parameter to be optimized. Therefore,

\begin{equation}
    S = c\left(r_{\mathrm{tip}} - r_{\mathrm{root}}\right),
    \label{eq:hydrofoil_area}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
    r_{\mathrm{root}}(c) = r_{\mathrm{tip}} - \frac{S}{c}.
    \label{eq:rroot_function_c}
\end{equation}

Here, $c$ denotes the designed chord length of the hydrofoil, rather than the instantaneous submerged chord length. During water entry and exit, the hydrofoil may only be partially submerged. If the penetration depth is denoted by $z$, the effective submerged chord length is approximated as

\begin{equation}
    c_{\mathrm{sub}}
    =
    \min\left(\frac{z}{\sin{\beta}}, c\right),
    \quad
    0 \leq c_{\mathrm{sub}} \leq c.
    \label{eq:submerged_chord_used}
\end{equation}

Thus, in the force calculation, the blade element area is written as

\begin{equation}
    dS_{\mathrm{sub}} = c_{\mathrm{sub}}\,dr .
    \label{eq:submerged_area_element}
\end{equation}

Substituting the differential lift and drag into the force decomposition, the total vertical thrust generated by one hydrofoil is

\begin{equation}
    T_{\mathrm{foil}}(c)
    =
    \int_{r_{\mathrm{tip}}-\frac{S}{c}}^{r_{\mathrm{tip}}}
    \frac{1}{2}\rho c_{\mathrm{sub}} v^2(r)
    \left[
    C_L(\alpha(r))\cos{\gamma(r)}
    +
    C_D(\alpha(r))\sin{\gamma(r)}
    \right] dr .
    \label{eq:total_thrust_c}
\end{equation}

Similarly, the resisting torque generated by one hydrofoil is

\begin{equation}
    Q_{\mathrm{foil}}(c)
    =
    \int_{r_{\mathrm{tip}}-\frac{S}{c}}^{r_{\mathrm{tip}}}
    \frac{1}{2}\rho c_{\mathrm{sub}} r v^2(r)
    \left[
    C_D(\alpha(r))\cos{\gamma(r)}
    -
    C_L(\alpha(r))\sin{\gamma(r)}
    \right] dr .
    \label{eq:total_torque_c}
\end{equation}

If the vehicle has $N$ identical hydrofoils that contact the water under the same condition, the total vertical thrust and resisting torque are approximated as

\begin{equation}
    T = N T_{\mathrm{foil}}(c),
    \label{eq:total_vehicle_thrust}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
    Q = N Q_{\mathrm{foil}}(c).
    \label{eq:total_vehicle_torque}
\end{equation}

In practice, $N$ represents the number of effective hydrofoils in contact with the water at a given time step.

Combining the above equations, the total vertical thrust and resisting torque can be summarized as nonlinear functions of the vehicle states and hydrofoil design parameters:

\begin{equation}
    T =
    T\left(
    z,\dot{z},\omega,
    c,S,r_{\mathrm{tip}},
    \beta,N,\rho
    \right),
    \label{eq:T_function}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
    Q =
    Q\left(
    z,\dot{z},\omega,
    c,S,r_{\mathrm{tip}},
    \beta,N,\rho
    \right).
    \label{eq:Q_function}
\end{equation}

The vertical and rotational dynamics of the vehicle during water impact are then written as

\begin{equation}
    m\ddot{z}
    =
    T\left(
    z,\dot{z},\omega,
    c,S,r_{\mathrm{tip}},
    \beta,N,\rho
    \right)
    - mg,
    \label{eq:vertical_dynamics_function}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
    I\dot{\omega}
    =
    -Q\left(
    z,\dot{z},\omega,
    c,S,r_{\mathrm{tip}},
    \beta,N,\rho
    \right).
    \label{eq:rotational_dynamics_function}
\end{equation}

This formulation separates the hydrofoil design parameter $c$ from the instantaneous submerged chord length $c_{\mathrm{sub}}$. The design chord length determines the hydrofoil geometry and the radial integration range, while $c_{\mathrm{sub}}$ determines the instantaneous submerged area during water entry and exit.
