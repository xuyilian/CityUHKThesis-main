\chapter{Dynamic Modeling of Water-Skipping Motion}

\label{dynamic}

\section{Overview of Water-Skipping Process}

\section{Quasi-Steady Hydrodynamic Model}

During the water-skipping process, the rotating hydrofoil impacts the water surface and generates hydrodynamic lift and drag. Since the density of water is much larger than that of air, the hydrodynamic force during water contact dominates the motion of the vehicle. Therefore, in this model, only the force generated by the submerged hydrofoil is considered.

Figure~\ref{fig:model_definitions}(A) shows the quasi-steady hydrodynamic model of a hydrofoil during water impact. The hydrofoil moves with a horizontal velocity component $v_x$, which is mainly induced by the rotation of the vehicle, and a vertical velocity component $\dot{z}$, which is caused by the falling motion of the vehicle. The resultant velocity of the hydrofoil relative to water is denoted as $v$.

\begin{figure}[t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{Assets/blade_element_diagram.pdf}
    \caption{Definitions for the water-skipping hydrodynamic model. \textbf{(A)} Side view (the $x$--$z$ plane) of a single foil section, showing the inclination angle $\beta$, the inflow angle $\gamma$, the angle of attack $\alpha = \beta + \gamma$, the relative velocity $v$ with its horizontal and vertical components $v_x$ and $\dot{z}$, and the lift and drag forces $F_L$ and $F_D$ (perpendicular and parallel to $v$). \textbf{(B)} Top view (the $x$--$y$ plane) of the rotating foil, spinning at angular rate $\omega$ about the marked axis of rotation and extending from that axis to the tip radius $r_{\mathrm{tip}}$; a blade element at radius $r$ has radial width $dr$. The chord $c$ denotes the physical streamwise extent of the foil; only its submerged portion $c_{\mathrm{sub}}$ enters the model.}
    \label{fig:model_definitions}
\end{figure}


The resultant velocity can be expressed as

\begin{equation}
    v = \sqrt{v_x^2 + \dot{z}^2}.
    \label{eq:resultant_velocity}
\end{equation}

The angle between the resultant velocity and the horizontal direction is defined as $\gamma$, which is given by

\begin{equation}
    \gamma = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{-\dot{z}}{v_x}\right).
    \label{eq:gamma}
\end{equation}

Here, $\dot{z}<0$ represents downward motion before impact. Therefore, $-\dot{z}$ is used to represent the downward velocity magnitude. The hydrofoil is installed with an inclination angle $\beta$ relative to the horizontal direction. As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:model_definitions}(A), the angle of attack $\alpha$ is determined by both the hydrofoil inclination angle and the incoming flow direction. Thus,

\begin{equation}
    \alpha = \beta + \gamma.
    \label{eq:alpha}
\end{equation}

Based on the quasi-steady assumption, the instantaneous hydrodynamic lift and drag generated by the hydrofoil can be written as

\begin{equation}
    F_L = \frac{1}{2}\rho C_L S v^2,
    \label{eq:lift_force}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
    F_D = \frac{1}{2}\rho C_D S v^2,
    \label{eq:drag_force}
\end{equation}

where $\rho$ is the density of water, $S$ is the submerged area of the hydrofoil, and $C_L$ and $C_D$ are the lift and drag coefficients, respectively. The lift force $F_L$ is perpendicular to the relative flow direction, while the drag force $F_D$ acts along the direction opposite to the relative flow.

For a flat-plate hydrofoil, the lift and drag coefficients can be approximated by

\begin{equation}
    C_L = 2 \sin{\alpha}\cos{\alpha},
    \label{eq:cl}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
    C_D = 2 \sin^2{\alpha},
    \label{eq:cd}
\end{equation}

\section{Blade Element Method}

The quasi-steady hydrodynamic model describes the lift and drag generated by a hydrofoil with a given velocity and angle of attack. However, for a rotating hydrofoil, the linear velocity is not uniform along the span, since each section has a different distance from the rotational center. Therefore, the blade element method is used to calculate the hydrodynamic force distribution along the hydrofoil.

As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:model_definitions}(B), the hydrofoil is mounted on the rotating body and extends from the axis of rotation to the tip radius $r_{\mathrm{tip}}$. A small blade element located at radius $r$ has a radial length $dr$. The tangential velocity, resultant velocity, inflow angle, and local angle of attack of the blade element are given by

\begin{equation}
    v_x(r) = \omega r,
    \label{eq:blade_tangential_velocity}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
    v(r) = \sqrt{(\omega r)^2 + \dot{z}^{\,2}},
    \label{eq:blade_resultant_velocity}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
    \gamma(r) = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{-\dot{z}}{\omega r}\right),
    \label{eq:blade_gamma}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
    \alpha(r) = \beta + \gamma(r),
    \label{eq:blade_alpha}
\end{equation}

where $\omega$ is the angular velocity of the vehicle, $\dot{z}$ is the vertical velocity, and $\beta$ is the hydrofoil inclination angle.

Because the hydrofoil is a thin slanted plate, the portion submerged at penetration depth $z$ extends a length $z/\sin{\beta}$ along the chordwise (slant) direction. This wetted length grows with depth only until the plate is fully wetted, which occurs at the depth $h^{*} = c\sin{\beta}$, where $c$ is the physical chord of the foil; at greater depths the wetted length is bounded by the chord itself. The submerged chord is therefore

\begin{equation}
    c_{\mathrm{sub}} = \min\!\left(\frac{z}{\sin{\beta}},\; c\right),
    \label{eq:submerged_chord}
\end{equation}

and is the same for every blade element at a given instant. The submerged area of a blade element of radial length $dr$ is then

\begin{equation}
    dS = c_{\mathrm{sub}}\,dr .
    \label{eq:blade_area}
\end{equation}

Based on the quasi-steady assumption, the differential lift and drag generated by this blade element are

\begin{equation}
    dF_L = \frac{1}{2}\rho C_L(\alpha) v^2(r)\,c_{\mathrm{sub}}\,dr ,
    \label{eq:differential_lift}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
    dF_D = \frac{1}{2}\rho C_D(\alpha) v^2(r)\,c_{\mathrm{sub}}\,dr ,
    \label{eq:differential_drag}
\end{equation}

where $\rho$ is the density of water, and $C_L(\alpha)$ and $C_D(\alpha)$ are the lift and drag coefficients.

The differential vertical thrust and horizontal resistance force are obtained by decomposing the lift and drag forces:

\begin{equation}
    dT = dF_L \cos{\gamma(r)} + dF_D \sin{\gamma(r)} ,
    \label{eq:differential_thrust}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
    dF_R = dF_D \cos{\gamma(r)} - dF_L \sin{\gamma(r)} .
    \label{eq:differential_resistance}
\end{equation}

The horizontal resistance force produces a resisting torque about the rotational center:

\begin{equation}
    dQ = r\,dF_R
    =
    r\left(dF_D \cos{\gamma(r)} - dF_L \sin{\gamma(r)}\right).
    \label{eq:differential_torque}
\end{equation}


\section{Vertical Motion and Rotational Dynamics}

The total vertical thrust and resisting torque generated by one hydrofoil are obtained by integrating the blade elements along the radial direction, from the axis of rotation ($r=0$) to the tip radius $r_{\mathrm{tip}}$. Because the foil begins at the axis, its radial span equals $r_{\mathrm{tip}}$, and this tip radius is the primary geometric design variable. The chord $c$ enters the model only as the saturation limit of the submerged chord $c_{\mathrm{sub}}$ of~\eqref{eq:submerged_chord}: in the shallow phase of the contact the wetted length is set by the penetration depth alone, while in the deeper phase it is bounded by the physical extent of the foil.

Substituting the differential lift and drag into the force decomposition, the total vertical thrust generated by one hydrofoil is

\begin{equation}
    T_{\mathrm{foil}}
    =
    \int_{0}^{r_{\mathrm{tip}}}
    \frac{1}{2}\rho c_{\mathrm{sub}} v^2(r)
    \left[
    C_L(\alpha(r))\cos{\gamma(r)}
    +
    C_D(\alpha(r))\sin{\gamma(r)}
    \right] dr .
    \label{eq:total_thrust_c}
\end{equation}

Similarly, the resisting torque generated by one hydrofoil is

\begin{equation}
    Q_{\mathrm{foil}}
    =
    \int_{0}^{r_{\mathrm{tip}}}
    \frac{1}{2}\rho c_{\mathrm{sub}} r v^2(r)
    \left[
    C_D(\alpha(r))\cos{\gamma(r)}
    -
    C_L(\alpha(r))\sin{\gamma(r)}
    \right] dr .
    \label{eq:total_torque_c}
\end{equation}

If the vehicle has $N$ identical hydrofoils that contact the water under the same condition, the total vertical thrust and resisting torque are approximated as

\begin{equation}
    T = N T_{\mathrm{foil}},
    \label{eq:total_vehicle_thrust}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
    Q = N Q_{\mathrm{foil}}.
    \label{eq:total_vehicle_torque}
\end{equation}

In practice, $N$ represents the number of effective hydrofoils in contact with the water at a given time step.

Combining the above equations, the total vertical thrust and resisting torque can be summarized as nonlinear functions of the vehicle states and hydrofoil design parameters:

\begin{equation}
    T =
    T\left(
    z,\dot{z},\omega,
    r_{\mathrm{tip}},c,
    \beta,N,\rho
    \right),
    \label{eq:T_function}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
    Q =
    Q\left(
    z,\dot{z},\omega,
    r_{\mathrm{tip}},c,
    \beta,N,\rho
    \right).
    \label{eq:Q_function}
\end{equation}

The vertical and rotational dynamics of the vehicle during water impact are then written as

\begin{equation}
    m\ddot{z}
    =
    T\left(
    z,\dot{z},\omega,
    r_{\mathrm{tip}},c,
    \beta,N,\rho
    \right)
    - mg,
    \label{eq:vertical_dynamics_function}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
    I\dot{\omega}
    =
    -Q\left(
    z,\dot{z},\omega,
    r_{\mathrm{tip}},c,
    \beta,N,\rho
    \right).
    \label{eq:rotational_dynamics_function}
\end{equation}

In this formulation the hydrofoil geometry enters through two parameters: the tip radius $r_{\mathrm{tip}}$, which sets the radial integration range $0 \le r \le r_{\mathrm{tip}}$ (the radial span coincides with $r_{\mathrm{tip}}$, since the foil extends from the axis of rotation, and is taken as the design variable), and the chord $c$, which bounds the chordwise wetting. The submerged chord $c_{\mathrm{sub}} = \min(z/\sin{\beta},\,c)$ grows with the penetration depth in the shallow phase of the contact and saturates at the physical chord once the plate is fully wetted, at depths beyond $h^{*} = c\sin{\beta}$.
\section{Simulation}
\label{sec:simulation}

The coupled vertical and rotational dynamics of~\eqref{eq:vertical_dynamics_function} and~\eqref{eq:rotational_dynamics_function} are integrated numerically through a single water contact. Starting from the instant the foils touch the surface, the state $(z,\dot{z},\omega)$ is advanced with a fixed-step semi-implicit Euler scheme until the foils leave the water---that is, until $z$ returns to the surface with an upward velocity. For a given entry condition (entry spin $\omega_0$ and downward entry velocity $\dot{z}_0$) and foil design (tip radius $r_{\mathrm{tip}}$ and inclination $\beta$), the simulation returns the two quantities that characterise the hop: the exit spin $\omega_{\mathrm{exit}}$ and the exit vertical velocity $\dot{z}_{\mathrm{exit}}$.

\paragraph{Anatomy of a hop.}
Figure~\ref{fig:hop_time_history} overlays nine simulated contacts that combine three foil inclinations ($\beta = 25^\circ, 45^\circ, 65^\circ$) with three entry speeds ($\dot{z}_0 = -1, -3, -5~\mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}$), all entering at $\omega_0 = 3500~\mathrm{deg\,s^{-1}}$ with a tip radius of $45~\mathrm{mm}$. In each case the solid curve is the vertical velocity $\dot{z}(t)$ (left axis) and the dashed curve the spin $\omega(t)$ (right axis); the cases are separated into the two panels by outcome, with the five contacts that return to the air on the left and the four that do not on the right.

The successful contacts (left) are the three $\beta = 25^\circ$ cases together with the two gentler $\beta = 45^\circ$ cases. Each arrests the descent within the first few tens of milliseconds and leaves the water (filled circles) after $55$--$185~\mathrm{ms}$, with an upward exit velocity of $0.2$--$0.5~\mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}$ and an exit spin between roughly $1700$ and $3150~\mathrm{deg\,s^{-1}}$---comfortably above the $1000~\mathrm{deg\,s^{-1}}$ controller floor; steeper inclination and faster entry pay for their larger hydrodynamic loads with longer contacts and heavier spin loss. The failures (right) come in two kinds. The steep, fast contacts ($\beta = 65^\circ$ at $-3$ and $-5~\mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}$) exhaust the spin entirely while still submerged (crosses at $40$--$85~\mathrm{ms}$) and can never be ejected. The marginal contacts ($\beta = 65^\circ$ at $-1~\mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}$ and $\beta = 45^\circ$ at $-5~\mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}$) do arrest the descent, but then linger just below the surface while the resisting torque bleeds the spin past the controller floor before the end of the $300~\mathrm{ms}$ window: the vehicle is no longer sinking, yet the hop has failed.

In every successful case the reversal is asymmetric: the descent is arrested quickly, but the climb back to the surface is slower and recovers only a fraction of the entry speed, because the spin that powers the lift decays during the contact. The water contact is therefore not a conservative bounce; its role is to \emph{reverse the vertical velocity and return the vehicle to the air with controllable spin}, while the propellers restore the remaining energy during the subsequent flight phase. The contrast between the moderate- and steep-$\beta$ contacts already points to the design compromise quantified next.

\paragraph{Design space and the hop--spin trade-off.}
Evaluating the model across the design space spanned by the inclination $\beta$, the tip radius $r_{\mathrm{tip}}$, and the entry velocity $\dot{z}_0$ shows how the two hop metrics depend on the design. Figure~\ref{fig:design_space_maps} plots every simulated design as a point in this space, coloured by the exit vertical velocity $\dot{z}_{\mathrm{exit}}$ (left, the hop) and by the exit spin $\omega_{\mathrm{exit}}$ (right, the retained spin).

The two maps peak in different regions. The hop is largest---approaching $0.7~\mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}$ of exit velocity---at moderate inclination, large tip radius, and a moderate entry velocity: too small an entry speed supplies little energy, while too large an entry speed drives the foils deep without a clean ejection. The retained spin, by contrast, is largest (above $3000~\mathrm{deg\,s^{-1}}$) at small inclination and gentle entry, where the foils do the least work against the spin. Because every change that enlarges the hop---steeper $\beta$, a larger foil, or faster entry---also loads the foils more heavily and bleeds more spin, the two objectives are in direct opposition.

This trade-off defines the design problem: maximise the hop subject to the exit spin remaining above the controllable minimum. The feasible optimum therefore lies at a compromise---moderate inclination and a large tip radius---where the hop is near its peak while the exit spin is still comfortably retained. The geometry and operating point selected on this basis are detailed in Chapter~\ref{chapter:paperone}.
